Quadrupedal Robot

Completed

The Beginning

During the initial stages of this project, a plethora of ideas were explored and brainstormed. The spectrum of possibilities stretched from envisioning a humanoid robot to conceiving a peculiar centipede-like creature. I invested considerable time in crafting concept drawings, conducting comprehensive gait analyses, and performing intricate calculations involving crucial factors like weight, torque, and speed. These calculations revolved around the gait patterns and components of the envisioned robot. Ultimately, after careful consideration, I settled on the idea of modeling the design after a bacteriophage. This decision was driven by a combination of its striking aesthetics and the original intention to create a three-legged robot. I believed that this unique choice would lend an otherworldly appearance to the robot as it moved, truly setting it apart from conventional designs.

The Middle

The process of designing the robot presented its fair share of challenges, with a significant portion of my time dedicated to perfecting the intricate spiral shape of the body. My CAD software initially struggled with this complex form, but I persisted until I discovered a solution. Once I had overcome this hurdle, crafting the remaining parts proved relatively straightforward. However, upon reaching the assembly stage, I encountered a discouraging realization: the robot would not be able to walk. Several factors contributed to this setback. Firstly, I had only incorporated a single type of rotational movement (either yaw or roll), which proved inadequate for the symmetrical nature of the robot. Secondly, the three-legged design posed difficulties as it relied on the exertion of force from a single leg to propel itself in a specific direction, resulting in slower and inefficient movement. Lastly, my design had inadvertently grown too large, rendering it excessively heavy for the motor to effectively propel. Determined to rectify these issues, I invested countless hours into redesigning the legs, optimizing weight distribution, and scaling down the overall size of the robot. With these adjustments made, I eagerly moved forward to the final stages of the projectâ€”printing the pieces and assembling them together.

The End

After investing weeks in 3D printing and utilizing SLS printing techniques with nylon and resin, I finally achieved the ideal balance of pieces that fit together seamlessly while remaining within a reasonable weight limit (under 3 pounds overall). The assembly process itself turned out to be surprisingly effortless, save for the occasional broken piece that required reprinting. Then came the moment I had been dreading: coding. However, contrary to my expectations, this step proved to be the easiest part of the entire project. Despite encountering some initial challenges with setup and intermittent SSH disconnects, I persevered and successfully programmed the bacteriophage-inspired robot, affectionately named Phage, to walk in four different directions at a steady pace of 15 cm/sec. Furthermore, Phage exhibits additional capabilities such as spinning, dancing, and waving. Looking ahead, my future plans for Phage involve incorporating voice activation using a microphone.
